# Section 2: Passive Components — Deep & Practical Edition

> **Before we start, a simple definition:** A "Passive" component is one that cannot amplify a signal or generate new energy on its own — it can only limit current, temporarily store energy, or reshape a signal. Think of it like a bucket, a pipe, or a weight — none of them have an engine, they just react. An "Active" component, on the other hand, like a transistor, can use a small signal to control a much larger one (we'll get to these in Section 3).

## 1. 🔧 Resistor

### What it is — using the Chapter 0 analogy

Remember in Chapter 0 we said a resistor is like **the narrowness of a doorway in a hallway** — the narrower the door, the harder it is for a crowd (current) to pass through. A resistor plays exactly this role in a real circuit: a two-pin component that deliberately creates an obstacle to current flow, just like we saw in Section 1 (LEDs, pull-ups, voltage dividers).

### Physical types of resistors

- **Carbon Composition/Film:** The oldest and cheapest type. Low precision, and its value drifts a bit over time or with rising temperature. It also produces more electrical noise (noise = small, unwanted voltage/current fluctuations generated by the component itself, not from an external source).
- **Metal Film:** The most common type in embedded projects today. Higher precision (1% tolerance is common), lower noise, better temperature stability. If you buy an ordinary resistor from a shop and it's light brown with colored bands, it's probably this type.
- **Wirewound:** For high power (several watts and up), a resistant metal wire is wound around a ceramic core. Bigger and pricier, but handles a lot of heat — you'll see these in motor brake circuits or dummy loads.
- **SMD vs THT:**
    - **THT (Through-Hole Technology):** A resistor with two wire legs that go through holes in the board and get soldered from the back — the kind you work with comfortably on a breadboard or perfboard.
    - **SMD (Surface-Mount Device):** A small rectangular chip with no legs, soldered directly onto the copper pads on the board's surface. Standard sizes are marked with a numeric code: **0402** (tiny, about 1×0.5mm, hard to hand-solder), **0603** and **0805** (medium size, best for beginner hand-soldering), **1206** (bigger, easiest to handle by hand). **Practical tip for your own project:** when you design your first custom PCB and plan to hand-solder it yourself, use 0805 or 1206, not 0402.
- **Different power ratings (1/8W, 1/4W, 1/2W, 1W...):** The higher the rated power, the physically bigger the resistor (since it needs more surface area to dissipate heat). This ties directly into the **Derating** concept from Section 1 — always keep your calculated real power at half of the resistor's rated power.

### Tolerance — why it matters

Tolerance means "how far the actual resistance can be from the number printed on it." A "100Ω resistor with 5% tolerance" means its real value can be anywhere between 95Ω and 105Ω.

**Why does it matter?** For a current-limiting resistor on an LED, that 5% difference doesn't matter at all — the human eye can't detect a 5% brightness difference. But for a voltage divider meant to precisely measure a battery's voltage (like the example in Section 1), that same 5% error can noticeably skew your final reading. **Rule of thumb:** wherever measurement accuracy matters (voltage reference circuits, precision dividers, precise filters), use 1% tolerance resistors; wherever you're just limiting current (LEDs, pull-ups), 5% tolerance is plenty and cheaper.

### Temperature Coefficient (TCR)

This spec tells you "how many parts per million (ppm) the resistance changes for every 1°C change in temperature." For example, a TCR of 100ppm/°C means a 10°C temperature rise changes the resistance by about 0.1%.

**Why does it matter?** In most hobby projects, it doesn't matter at all. But in precision analog circuits (like a precise voltage reference for sensor calibration, or a precision instrumentation amplifier) that need to work in variable-temperature environments (outdoors, like your own plant project), poor-TCR resistors will degrade the whole circuit's accuracy as the ambient temperature shifts — even if everything else is electrically correct.

### Color codes and standard values (E12/E24/E96 series)

Resistors are only manufactured in a standard set of values, not just any number you want. The reason is mathematical and industrial: if you want to cover the entire range of possible values with 10% tolerance using the fewest possible resistors (without too much overlap and without gaps), you end up with a specific logarithmic series — that's the E12 series (12 values per decade: 10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82).

- **E12** → typically used for 10% tolerance (12 values per decade).
- **E24** → for 5% tolerance (24 values per decade, denser since precision is higher).
- **E96** → for 1% tolerance (96 values per decade).

**This is exactly why, back in Section 1, when our calculation gave us 86.6Ω, we had to round to 100Ω** — the number 86.6 simply doesn't exist in any standard series.

**Reading color codes (a practical skill):** THT resistors have several colored bands. In the 4-band format: the first two bands are significant digits, the third is the multiplier (how many zeros to add), and the fourth is tolerance. For example, brown-black-red-gold = 1, 0, ×100 = 1000Ω (1kΩ) with 5% tolerance (gold). 🎨 Definitely practice this a few times with an online color chart until you can read it at a glance — in a real workplace, when you've got a few mixed boxes of resistors in front of you, this is often the fastest way to identify them (unless you happen to have a multimeter handy).

### Key real-world resistor applications you'll actually see in the field

- **Pull-up / Pull-down:** Full physics explanation was in Section 1.
- **LED current limiting:** Full explanation and numeric example was in Section 1.
- **Voltage divider:** Full explanation was in Section 1.
- **Bus termination:** Some communication protocols (like CAN) need a specific resistor (usually 120Ω for CAN) at both ends of the bus to prevent signal reflection on long wires — we'll cover this in full detail in Section 7 (Protocols).
- **Current sense / shunt resistor [Added]:** A very small resistor (like 0.1Ω or even 0.01Ω) deliberately placed in the path of a circuit's main current, purely so that by measuring the tiny voltage drop across it (via Ohm's law: V=IR), you can calculate the current flowing through — without breaking the circuit and without manually wiring in an ammeter in series. For example, with a 0.1Ω shunt and a 50mV drop across it: I = V/R = 0.05/0.1 = 0.5A. This is exactly the technique battery fuel-gauge circuits and overcurrent protection use.
- **Feedback resistor in adjustable regulators [Added]:** Many voltage regulators (like the famous LM317) set their output voltage using an external voltage divider (two resistors) — the exact same concept from Section 1, except this time, instead of an ADC reading the voltage, the IC itself uses that voltage to regulate its own output.
- **Snubber resistor:** Usually paired with a capacitor, used to dampen voltage spikes when a relay or transistor switches — full details in Section 5 (Protection Circuits).

### Real-world resistor selection checklist (what actually happens in an engineer's BOM)

When a senior engineer picks a resistor for a circuit, this is the thought process they go through:

1. **Value (ohms):** derived from the circuit's formula (like the LED example in Section 1).
2. **Rated power:** from calculating P=I²R, plus at least 2x derating.
3. **Tolerance:** does this circuit need high precision (1%) or is standard (5%) fine?
4. **Physical size (SMD/THT and size code):** based on available PCB space and assembly method (hand or machine).
5. **Availability in a standard series:** does the calculated value exist in E12/E24/E96, or does it need rounding?

## 2. ⚡ Capacitor

### What it is — using the Chapter 0 analogy

Remember we said a capacitor is like a **"waiting room with a mesh in the middle"** — charge builds up on one side and, through pressure (not direct passage), pushes on the other side. The bigger the room (capacitor), the more charge it can accumulate.

### Physical types of capacitors

- **Ceramic / MLCC (Multi-Layer Ceramic Capacitor):** The most common type in embedded work. Cheap, small, non-polarized (direction doesn't matter when connecting it), and ideal for decoupling capacitors. Important note though: ceramic capacitors come in different "dielectric classes" with very different behavior:
    - **C0G/NP0:** Very stable and precise (value stays essentially constant with temperature and voltage), but only available in small values (a few picofarads to a few nanofarads). Used for precision timing circuits (like the crystal load capacitors we'll see in Section 4).
    - **X7R:** Medium to large values (up to a few microfarads), but the real value shifts somewhat with temperature and voltage (around 15%). Perfectly fine and the most common choice for general decoupling.
    - **Y5V/Z5U:** Cheapest, highest capacitance in the smallest package, but the real value can be 50-80% lower than the printed number (especially under high DC voltage or high temperature)! **Important practical note:** never use this class for critical decoupling or precise filtering — only use it where the exact value doesn't matter (like a very rough filter).
- **Electrolytic:** High capacitance (a few microfarads to several thousand microfarads) in a relatively small volume, but **polarized** (direction matters — the negative pin is usually marked with a stripe on the body). Used for bulk energy storage, not fast decoupling, since its internal resistance (ESR) is relatively high.
- **Tantalum:** Polarized like electrolytic but more stable and precise, in a smaller size. **Important safety warning:** unlike other capacitors that just fail quietly, if you connect a tantalum backwards (wrong polarity) or exceed its rated voltage, **it can suddenly short-circuit and even spark or catch fire** — always handle it with more caution than an electrolytic.
- **Film Capacitor:** Non-polarized, very stable, but physically larger than an equivalent ceramic. Mostly seen in precision audio circuits, AC filters, or places where long-term stability matters — you'll rarely need one in typical embedded projects.

### ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance) — a concept many people only know by name

Every real capacitor, besides its capacitance, also has an unwanted internal resistance (due to the plate material and internal electrolyte) — this is called **ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance)**. The lower the ESR, the "cleaner" and "faster" the capacitor behaves.

**Why does it matter?** When a large current passes through a capacitor quickly (like at the output of a switching converter, or decoupling for a fast IC), this ESR generates heat and an extra voltage drop — exactly like a battery's internal resistance causing its voltage to sag under heavy current draw. **Rule of thumb:** for decoupling and switching-converter output filtering, always prefer a low-ESR capacitor (usually ceramic); ordinary electrolytics have higher ESR and aren't sufficient alone for these applications (which is why a small ceramic is often placed alongside a large electrolytic).

### Rated voltage and capacitor derating [Added]

Every capacitor has a "maximum rated voltage" (printed on the body, like 16V or 25V) — meaning you should never apply more than this voltage, or the internal dielectric will break down, shorting or damaging the capacitor.

**Derating rule of thumb:** just like with resistors, it's recommended to pick a capacitor with a rated voltage at least 1.5 to 2x the actual circuit voltage. For example, on a 5V rail, use a 16V capacitor (not exactly 6.3V).

**An interesting advanced note few people know:** ceramic capacitors (especially X7R and Y5V) lose a noticeable chunk of their real capacitance when operated near a relatively high DC voltage (close to their rated voltage) — this is called DC Bias Derating. So a "10µF/16V" capacitor actually operating at 5V might only deliver 6-7µF in practice. That's why in precision designs, experienced engineers always pick a capacitor's rated voltage well above the actual operating voltage, not just slightly above.

### Decoupling/bypass capacitors — from a component-selection angle

We covered the full physics of this concept in Section 1 (why 100nF, why close to the IC). Here we look at it from "which physical capacitor should I pick": always choose **ceramic** for decoupling (low ESR, fast response), not electrolytic. The X7R dielectric class is sufficient for most cases.

**A practical detail you'll see in professional designs:** on many boards, you'll notice several decoupling capacitors of different values next to each IC (like 100nF + 10nF + 1µF together). Here's why: each capacitance value covers a different frequency range better (smaller capacitors respond faster to very-high-frequency noise, larger ones handle slower, bigger fluctuations). Combining several values side by side covers a wider spectrum of possible noise.

### Bulk capacitor vs. decoupling

A bulk capacitor (usually electrolytic, tens to hundreds of microfarads) sits at the input of each power rail on a board — its job is storing energy for **slow, large** fluctuations (like the moment the whole board suddenly powers up, or a motor turns on), not very fast pulses. Decoupling (100nF near each IC) is for fast, local pulses. The two complement each other rather than replacing one another.

### RC time constant — a practical reminder

In Section 1 we fully covered how τ=RC determines charge/discharge time. One more practical application worth adding: an **RC low-pass filter** has a specific cutoff frequency, given by this formula:

```
f_cutoff = 1 / (2πRC)
```

Meaning frequencies below this number pass through easily, while frequencies above it get attenuated. This is exactly the circuit used to "smooth" a PWM output (mentioned in Section 1) or to remove high-frequency noise from an analog sensor's output.

### Other capacitor applications you'll see in the field

- **Coupling capacitor [Added]:** When you want to pass only the **varying (AC)** part of a signal from one stage to the next while blocking its constant (DC) part, you place a capacitor in series along the signal path. Why does this work? Exactly because of the reactance concept from Section 1: a capacitor acts like an open circuit at DC (the constant part doesn't pass), but at the signal's varying frequency, it behaves like an almost-open path (the varying part passes through). Common use: separating the DC level between two analog circuits with different reference voltages (bias), such as between two audio amplifier stages.
- **Timing capacitor in RC oscillator circuits:** for building an approximate frequency without a crystal (lower precision but cheap and simple).
- **Snubber capacitor:** paired with a resistor, to dampen voltage spikes when a relay/transistor switches (details in Section 5).
- **Hardware debounce capacitor:** fully explained in Section 1.

### Common mistakes in capacitor selection and use

- Using Y5V-class capacitors for critical decoupling (real capacitance ends up far below the printed value).
- Connecting an electrolytic or tantalum capacitor with reversed polarity.
- Choosing a rated voltage too close to the circuit's actual voltage (no derating).
- Using only one large electrolytic capacitor instead of pairing it with a small ceramic one for fast decoupling.

## 3. 🌀 Inductor

### What it is — a new analogy (since inductors behave differently from capacitors)

If a capacitor was like a "waiting room" that resists **changes in voltage**, an inductor is exactly the opposite: it resists **changes in current**. A good analogy: imagine trying to spin up a **heavy flywheel** — it's hard to get it started (you need to put in a lot of energy to get it up to speed), but once it's spinning, it's also hard to stop (it wants to keep spinning on its own). An inductor works the same way: it resists a **sudden start** of current, but once current is flowing and you suddenly try to cut it off, the inductor "resists" and tries to keep the current going with a voltage spike (this is exactly why we'll need a flyback diode in Section 5).

The physics: an inductor stores energy in a **magnetic field** (not in electric charge like a capacitor), by winding a wire around an iron or ferrite core.

### Role in switching converters (Buck/Boost)

In a Buck converter (step-down), a transistor rapidly switches current on and off, and the inductor "smooths" these chopped-up current pulses into a nearly continuous current — exactly like a flywheel absorbing the energy from each push and keeping the motion steady, rather than stopping and restarting with every pulse.

### EMI filters and chokes

Remember in Section 1 we said inductive reactance (Xl) **increases** with frequency? This is exactly the opposite of a capacitor. That's why an inductor plays the reverse role of a capacitor in EMI filtering: at DC it behaves like a plain wire (nearly zero resistance, letting the main current pass easily), but at high frequency (noise) it shows high resistance and blocks the noise from passing. An inductor used specifically for this job (noise filtering, not energy storage in a converter) is called a **choke**.

### Core saturation — the most important practical point in this section

The core inside an inductor (usually ferrite or iron) can only "hold" a magnetic field up to a certain limit. Picture a sponge absorbing water — it absorbs up to a point, but once fully soaked, it can't absorb any more, and extra water just runs off, as if the sponge weren't even there.

The exact same thing happens to an inductor's core: once the current passing through exceeds a certain limit (called the **saturation current, or Isat**), the core "saturates" and stops behaving like an inductor — effectively all that's left is the resistance of the internal wire (which is very low). Result: current suddenly spikes up sharply (since nothing's holding it back anymore), which can severely overheat the inductor and even damage the entire switching converter.

**Why picking the wrong inductor damages a converter:** if you mistakenly pick an inductor with Isat=1.5A for a Buck converter meant to deliver 2A, then at high load currents the core saturates, the converter loses control of the current, and typically either the inductor itself overheats or the converter's switching transistor gets damaged. **Rule of thumb:** always pick an inductor whose Isat is at least 20-30% higher than the circuit's actual maximum current.

### Other key specs

- **DCR (DC Resistance):** the resistance of the wire wound inside the inductor itself (never truly zero, no matter how small). Lower DCR means less loss and heat, and higher converter efficiency.
- **Shielded vs. Unshielded:** shielded inductors keep more of their magnetic field contained within themselves (less noise radiating to nearby components), but they're pricier and sometimes bulkier. In EMI-sensitive designs (near an antenna or sensitive analog circuit), shielded is preferred.

## 4. ⏱️ Crystal / Resonator

### Why we even need this component

In Section 1 we said a microcontroller needs a precise "clock" to operate — a very regular, repeating signal that acts like the system's heartbeat, keeping every operation in sync.

### Quartz crystal — the physics in plain language

Some materials (like quartz) have a strange property: apply voltage to them, and they physically **bend/compress** slightly; physically press on them, and they generate voltage on their own (this is called the "piezoelectric effect"). When a piece of quartz is cut into a specific shape, this stretch/return happens at a **very precise, stable frequency** — exactly like a tuning fork that always vibrates at the same specific note when struck, never a random one. A microcontroller's electronic circuit uses this precise vibration as its clock.

### Ceramic resonator — the cheaper, less-precise version

A resonator works on the same principle but is made from a ceramic material (not pure quartz) — cheaper, smaller, but less precise (more frequency error). It's fine for applications that don't need extreme timing accuracy (like a simple remote control), but for fast or sensitive communication (like USB), a real crystal is usually preferred.

### Load Capacitance — something a lot of people get wrong

A crystal's actual oscillation frequency doesn't depend on the crystal alone — it depends on the **total capacitance the crystal "sees"** (including the two small external capacitors usually placed beside it on the board, plus the PCB's own parasitic capacitance). A crystal's datasheet gives a specific number called **Load Capacitance (CL)** (like 12pF or 18pF), and the external capacitors need to be chosen precisely to deliver this value.

**Why does this matter?** If you pick the wrong external capacitors (too small or too large relative to the value calculated from CL), the crystal's actual frequency shifts slightly (the crystal doesn't stop working, it just runs at a slightly wrong frequency). This small shift is irrelevant for a blinking LED, but for fast communication (like USB or high UART baud rates), it can cause communication errors.

**Approximate formula for calculating external capacitors from CL:**

```
C_external ≈ 2 × (CL − C_stray)
```

where C_stray (usually around 2-5pF) is the estimated parasitic capacitance of the PCB and pins themselves. For example, for CL=12pF: C_external ≈ 2×(12-3) = 18pF (rounded to the nearest available standard value).

### Rated frequency and tolerance (ppm)

Every crystal has a frequency tolerance printed on it, in **ppm (parts per million)** — for example, ±20ppm. This means the actual frequency's error relative to the rated frequency is at most 20 per one million (a very tiny amount). **Why it matters:** in high-baud-rate serial communication, if two communicating devices (like two microcontrollers) each have clocks with different errors, bit timing will drift out of sync over time and communication will break down — a crystal with lower ppm (more precise) reduces this risk.

## 5. 🔌 Transformer

### Galvanic isolation — the key concept

A transformer consists of two (or more) coils wound around a shared core, but with **no direct electrical connection** between them — energy transfers purely through a shared magnetic field. This property is called **Galvanic Isolation**: two circuits can exchange energy or signals without sharing a common GND, or even without being in direct electrical contact at all.

**Why does it matter?** This is exactly what guarantees electrical safety in power supplies connected to mains electricity (220V AC) — the high-voltage (dangerous) side is completely electrically separated from the low-voltage side (the one you handle with your hands); only magnetic energy is transferred.

### AC voltage conversion

A transformer can step AC voltage up or down, based on the turns ratio between the primary and secondary coils — this is the foundation of old-style (non-switching) power adapters. In modern embedded projects, where you're usually working with a USB adapter or battery, you rarely deal with transformers directly, but understanding them matters for grasping why isolated power supplies are safer.

## 6. 🧯 Fuse and PTC (Resettable Fuse)

### Ordinary fuse — single-use

A fuse is a thin wire designed so that if the current exceeds a certain limit, the heat generated **melts** it and breaks the circuit — exactly like a "willing sacrifice" that destroys itself so the rest of the circuit stays safe. Once it's blown, it has to be physically replaced.

- **Fast-blow fuse:** trips very quickly (milliseconds) — protects sensitive components against sudden short circuits.
- **Slow-blow/time-lag fuse:** allows brief current spikes to pass without tripping (like the inrush current when a motor or transformer first turns on), but trips if high current persists for longer. **Practical use:** for circuits that naturally have a brief current spike on startup (like a pump or DC motor in your own project), a slow-blow fuse is the better choice — otherwise, a fast-blow fuse will trip the moment it powers on.

### PTC (resettable fuse / Polyfuse)

A PTC is made from a polymer material whose resistance rises sharply with increasing temperature. When current exceeds the allowed limit, the PTC itself heats up from that same excess current, its resistance suddenly jumps to a few hundred times higher (almost like a switch opening), and it sharply limits the current. Once the problem clears and the PTC cools down, it returns on its own to a low-resistance state — **with no need for physical replacement**, unlike an ordinary fuse.

**Analogy:** like a protective muscle that tenses and stiffens when it senses too much pressure, to prevent further damage, and relaxes again once the pressure is gone.

**Common use:** USB ports (protection against a possible short circuit in the connected device), battery protection circuits.

## 7. 🎛️ Potentiometer & Trimmer

Both are a **three-pin variable resistor**: two fixed ends (like an ordinary resistor) and a third pin called the "wiper" that slides along and varies the resistance between itself and each end — exactly like a voltage divider where one of the two resistors is adjustable.

- **Potentiometer:** designed for frequent adjustment by the end user — like a radio's volume knob that you turn every time.
- **Trimmer (trimmer potentiometer):** smaller, usually adjusted only with a screwdriver, designed for "one-time calibration at the factory or during repair" — not for everyday user adjustment.

**Practical embedded use:** manually setting an analog circuit's reference voltage, calibrating the sensitivity of an old-style analog sensor (like some gas sensors), or adjusting brightness/contrast on older LCD displays.

## 8. 🌡️ NTC/PTC Thermistor — temperature-dependent resistance [Added]

This shares the same physical principle as the "PTC fuse" from Section 6 (resistance depending on temperature), but its application is completely different: here the goal is **measuring temperature**, not protecting against current.

- **NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient):** resistance **decreases** as temperature rises. The most common thermistor type for temperature sensing in embedded projects.
- **PTC thermistor:** resistance **increases** as temperature rises (similar principle to the PTC fuse, but here used as a sensor or self-regulating heating element, not just protection).

**Practical use:** exactly like the voltage-divider example in Section 1 — you wire an NTC in series with a fixed resistor (voltage divider), read the midpoint voltage with an ADC, and since the NTC's resistance-temperature relationship is known (given in the datasheet as a table or the Steinhart-Hart formula), you calculate the actual temperature from the voltage reading. This is the cheapest and most common temperature-sensing method in hobbyist and semi-professional projects, before moving on to pricier digital sensors like the DS18B20.

| Component | First question to ask yourself |
|---|---|

## 10. ⚠️ Common mistakes across this whole section

- Not rounding a calculated resistance to the nearest standard series value (E12/E24/E96), wrongly assuming any number is available.
- Using Y5V-class capacitors where capacitance accuracy matters.
- Connecting a polarized capacitor (electrolytic/tantalum) backwards.
- Choosing an inductor with an Isat too close to the circuit's actual current (no safety margin).
- Ignoring load capacitance when selecting a crystal's external capacitors.
- Using a fast-blow fuse in a circuit that naturally has inrush current (like a motor), causing false trips.
